    Prediting a user's next order using tf-idf

    Food is an important part of everyone's life—and a big market. The average US household spends up to 33% of their income on groceries. Recently, Instacart—a grocery delivery service—open-sourced over 3 million of their user orders. This provided me a unique opportunity to study how people buy food, and see if we can predict what a shopper will buy on their next shopping trip.

    The prediction system displayed here works by comparing a user's past purchases with that of other users. First, the user's products are weighed by their importance using inverse-document frequency (idf). It then assembles a group of people deemed similar to the user, finds products with the highest cumulative weight, and recommends these products to the user.


    Vinny's orders are compared to other users in the database. It is observed that Vinny shares some similar products—white cheddar popcorn and Greek yogurt—with Austin. Similarly, Abby also likes to buy soda and beef jerky. The recommendation engine will recommend products that Abby and Austin like to Vinny.

    The purpose of the system is to predict what product a user will purchase on their next order. The model is able to predict 23% of the items a user will buy base on their order history. This is a 329% increase in accuracy when compared to a simple popular product recommender.


    While the demo here acts as a food recommender to a user, this algorithm is really at home in a shopping interface—where recommended items are surfaced to the user as they shop. Being able to accurately predict what a user will buy and recommend to them items that they might like simultaneously improves the user experience while driving up sales of these items.
